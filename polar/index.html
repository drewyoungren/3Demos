Polar coordinates \((r,\theta)\) specify a point in the plane by the formula: \begin{align*} x &= r \cos \theta \\ y &=r \sin \theta \end{align*}

That is, \(r\) measures distance from the origin and \(\theta\) measures angle in radians off the positive \(x\)-axis counterclockwise. We thus have the important relation $$r^2 = x^2 + y^2.$$

A polar rectangle is a region with coordinate bounds \(r_1 \leq r \leq r_2\) and \(\theta_1 \leq \theta \leq \theta_2\). Its area is given by $$\Delta A = \frac12 (\theta_2-\theta_1)(r_2^2 - r_1^2) = \overline{r}\, \Delta r\, \Delta \theta $$ where \(\overline{r} = (r_2 + r_1)/2\) is the average radius and \(\Delta r = (r_2^2 - r_1^2), \Delta \theta = (\theta_2^2 - \theta_1^2)\).

This gives the important result $$dA = r\,dr\,d\theta$$ for writing double integrals in polar coordinates.
